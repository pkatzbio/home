About

I am the Clinical Ethics Fellow at University Hospitals Cleveland Medical Center and Lecturer in Bioethics at Case Western Reserve University School of Medicine. My path to clinical ethics combines philosophy, literature, and embodied practice.

Before joining University Hospitals, I developed interdisciplinary bioethics curricula at California Northstate University and directed the Honors Program at Pacific Union College, where I taught medical humanities, philosophy of language, and ethics to pre-health students. I hold a Ph.D. in English from Syracuse University and an M.A. in Bioethics from The Ohio State University.

My research addresses epistemic injustice in clinical settings: how do power dynamics, language barriers, and implicit bias shape whose suffering is recognized and acted upon? I develop frameworks for moral decision-making under uncertainty, exploring how clinicians can cultivate dispositions toward epistemic humility and just care.

I've been practicing martial arts since age six, training eclectically across American Kenpo, Taekwondo, Aikido, Shotokan, and Brazilian Jiujitsu. This embodied practice deeply informs my scholarship on how physical training cultivates particular orientations toward the world and others—a thread that runs through both my clinical ethics work and my role as Editor-in-Chief of Martial Arts Studies.

Clinical Ethics

As Clinical Ethics Fellow at University Hospitals Cleveland Medical Center, I provide ethics consultation across the hospital system, collaborate on policy development, and serve on multiple ethics committees including Pediatric Ethics and Patient-Without-Proxy.

My clinical work focuses on epistemic justice: ensuring that marginalized patients' perspectives are heard and valued in healthcare decision-making. I'm particularly interested in cases involving chronic pain, disability, end-of-life care, and resource allocation—contexts where power asymmetries often determine whose knowledge counts.

Teaching

I believe ethics education should be experiential, reflective, and deeply connected to students' future practice. My teaching combines flipped classroom videos, self-grading rubrics that cultivate metacognition, and essay assignments that invite students to explore how their values will shape their professional lives.

Flipped Classroom

I create short instructional videos that students watch before class, freeing class time for discussion, case analysis, and collaborative problem-solving.

View teaching videos →

Reflective Assessment

Students use self-grading rubrics to assess their own work, developing metacognitive skills and taking ownership of their learning.

Values in Practice

Essay assignments ask pre-health students to articulate how their personal values will inform their future clinical practice—bridging ethics and professional identity.
